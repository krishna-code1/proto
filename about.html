About Biodiesel

Understanding the Future of Clean Energy

What is Biodiesel?

Biodiesel is a renewable, biodegradable fuel manufactured domestically from vegetable oils, animal fats, or recycled restaurant grease. It is a cleaner-burning replacement for petroleum diesel fuel.

Biodiesel meets both the biomass-based diesel and overall advanced biofuel requirement of the Renewable Fuel Standard. Renewable diesel, a fuel produced from the same fats and oils, also qualifies for these categories.

The chemical process used to produce biodiesel is called transesterification, where glycerin is separated from the fat or vegetable oil. This process leaves behind two products ‚Äî methyl esters (biodiesel) and glycerin (a valuable byproduct used in soaps and other products).

History of Biodiesel

1893

The Beginning

Rudolf Diesel's first engine ran on peanut oil at the World Exhibition in Paris, demonstrating that vegetable oils could power compression-ignition engines.

1930s-1940s

Early Experiments

During World War II, vegetable oils were used as diesel fuel substitutes when petroleum supplies were restricted, proving their viability as an emergency fuel source.

1970s

Oil Crisis Response

The 1970s energy crisis sparked renewed interest in alternative fuels. Scientists began developing the transesterification process to create biodiesel from vegetable oils.

1990s

Commercial Production

The first commercial biodiesel plants opened in Europe. The U.S. followed with the National Biodiesel Board establishing standards and promoting the industry.

2000s-Present

Global Expansion

Biodiesel production has grown exponentially worldwide. Government mandates and environmental regulations have driven adoption, with production reaching billions of gallons annually.
